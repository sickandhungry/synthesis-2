The Evolution of Chance:

An analysis of key themes in 10 Day, Acid Rap, and Coloring Book

In just a few years, Chance the Rapper has gone from a normal high school student to an international celebrity. Recently he became the first independent artist to win a Grammy Award, with Coloring Book winning 2017 Rap Album of the Year.
Chance has been through a lot since his first album was released, from a taxing drug addiction to the birth of his daughter. As he has grown as an artist and as a person, the themes of his albums have changed substantially over time. This project highlights some of the major themes of his three major mixtapes.
These charts are designed to measure relativity between different songs rather than total occurences; each chart is scaled to make the dots appear as large as possible. Color differences are purely aesthetic.

10 Day was Chance's first full-length mixtape. The songs were written during Chance's final days high school, while he was serving on a 10-day suspension for possessing marijuana on campus. After writing the songs of 10 Day, Chance dropped out of school to begin working on the album full-time. One line explains, "Fun fact: I'm never going back to school. Been there, done that."

In 10 Day, Chance raps about his transition from everyday student to local celebrity. In Long Time Part II, he sings, "Things ain’t been the same since Ms. Patterson called me famous." In Prom Night, he rapped about missing events like prom and graduation, and listening to teachers sneer at his musical dreams: "You made a mixtape? Good job, I hope you get a good job." At this point, Chance had a lot to prove to his teachers, family, peers.

This album detailed Chance's life on the South Side of Chicago, with references to money, cops, and guns. However, it didn't portray the gangster lifestyle in a very positive light; in Missing You, Chance raps, "Brown boys are dying and none of 'em were for business, and, all of 'em love they mamas and all of they mamas miss 'em." This was the beginning of Chance's long fight against violence in the streets of Chicago. More than anything, this was an album full of hope, for Chance's future as well as his city's.

Acid Rap was the album that jolted Chance into international fame; it was ranked 12 on Pitchfork's Top 50 Albums of 2013 and nominated for Best Mixtape at the 2013 BET Hip Hop Awards. Acid Rap was the genre-bender that put alternative hip hop on the map; Pitchfork wrote, "it invites elements of classic soul, juke, gospel, blues-rock, drill, acid jazz, house, ragtime scat, and R. Kelly, Twista, and a young Kanye to the same open mic poetry night, where the kid on-stage is declaiming about what’s going unreported."

On the surface, Acid Rap was an album about sex and drugs. Acid is mentioned 26 times on the album, in six different songs. The album's biggest hit Cocoa Butter Kisses mentions cigarettes, absinthe, peyote, weed, codeine, methamphetamine all within five minutes, with a line about putting "Visine inside my eyes so my grandma would fucking hug me" and a chorus ending with "I think we all addicted."

However, Acid Rap was more than meets the eye. On Acid Rain, the verse that begins with "kicked off my shoes, tripped acid in the rain, wore my jacket as a cape, and my umbrella as a cane" ends with "My big homie died young; just turned older than him. I seen it happen, I seen it happen, I see it always. He still be screaming, I see his demons in empty hallways." Paranoia builds on this theme, with the lines, "They murking kids, they murder kids here. Why you think they don't talk about it? They deserted us here."

Acid Rap is an album of pain - depression, addiction, and loss - juxtaposed with love. That's Love exclaims, "What's better than tripping, is falling in love. What's better than followers, is actually falling in love. What's better than rhymes, nickles, dimes, dollars, and dubs, is dialing up your darling just for calling her up. There ain't nothing better than falling in love." This juxtaposition is what makes the album so powerful - Acid Rap reminds us that no matter how bad things get, there's always love.

After the success of Acid Rap, "Chance 3" became one of the most highly anticipated albums of 2016. When Coloring Book was finally released, a lot of fans were surprised by what they heard. Coloring Book was a huge shift in themes and musical style from Chance's older music.

The album was full of very heavy gospel themes, which isolated a lot non-religious fans. The first single, Blessings includes a chorus, "When the praises go up, the blessings come down," and the lines, "I know them drugs isn't close, ain't no visit in heaven, I know the difference between blessings and worldly possessions." The mixtape even included a cover of How Great is Our God, with the lyrics, "Name above all names, worthy of all praise. My heart will sing, how great is our God... God is better than the best day that the world has to offer."

Another big change in Chance's life between Acid Rap and Coloring Book was the birth of his daughter Kensli. Chance has said that Coloring Book was written as a message to her, and she is referenced a few times in the mixtape. In Angels, Chance raps, "Clean up the streets, so my daughter can have somewhere to play."

Leading up to Coloring Book, Chance was inspired to ramp up his hometown activism. In 2015, he started a campaign that led Chicago - a city that averages 16 murders per day - to go 48 hours without any shootings. On Election Day 2016, Chance led a massive "Parade to the Polls" to encourage more young people to vote. In 2017, he met with the Governor of Illinois to demand more funding towards Chicago Public Schools, and shortly after announced a $1,000,000 donation to the cause.

Coloring Book, ultimately, was a mixtape about bringing people together to fight for their own liberation. Despite it's huge Christian themes, this was a message that spoke to people of all faiths.

Artist Statement

This was, without a doubt, one of the most complicated projects I’ve ever worked on. However, since it was based on a topic I’m passionate about, it was also one of the most fun. I listen to these albums almost everyday, and know most of the lyrics by heart. From start to finish, I really enjoyed solving different problems to create a final product that I could be proud of, and learning new things about my favorite artist along the way.
The data was gathered with my own Python program, the Chano-meter, which automatically counts the occurences of any given word in each of Chance's songs. The first step was creating the program. This was my first time working with Python outside of Codecademy and DataCamp tutorials. As a beginner, I even had trouble figuring out where I was supposed to save my code and how to run it in the terminal. After figuring that out, I spent a long time trying to get the count function to work with an example list.
Finally, I had a working counter. The next task was figuring out how to use that counter for actual lyrics. Originally, I pasted the entire lyrics for each song into my python code. After asking my friend for help, I realized this was a big “oh no”. My friend helped me link text files into the Python code and finish the program. I added some finishing touches, like creating categories for different albums and changing the words for user prompts, and was ready for the next step.
After finishing the program, I wasn’t sure what actual visualization I wanted to create. I considered a tag cloud but thought this wouldn’t be very interesting. After seeing this visualization on the Bokeh examples gallery, I decided I wanted to make a scatter plot. I thought a long time about how I wanted to design it, which data I wanted to display on each axis, and which colors I wanted to use. I ultimately decided that one of my biggest goals for this project was to make it look cool. Considering the topic, I wanted this project to look like a work of art.
My major challenge with the visualization was dealing with huge outliers. Most data points only represented one or two occurrences, but the largest (We/Our in All We Got) went up to 48 occurrences. This meant that if I wanted to include all these data points on the same sized plot, I would have to either make the largest point cover up the entire plot or make almost all of the points too small for the eye to see. This is why I decided to size the charts relatively. It allowed me to scale each chart with reasonably sized points. Another big challenge was trying to get rollovers to work, but I ran into errors because the charts were so unusually sized, and ultimately abandoned this feature.
After creating my first couple Bokeh charts, I began working on the actual HTML website. Once again, I decided that using Bootstrap was the easiest way to create a beautiful website. One challenge was creating columns to house the album art and the list. I had to try many different column styles to get it to look the way I wanted it to.
Finally, I finished the project by writing descriptions of each of the albums. In my descriptions, I combined bits and pieces of my data with facts about Chance’s life, reviews from other major media outlets, and of course, quotes from the actual songs. I feel like these were necessary to give meaning to all the complicated data, and I definitely had fun writing them.
